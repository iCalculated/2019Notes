Writing Workshop

Through the pieces of writing that the class has covered, it’s clear that communication has a fundamental part in both shaping and enforcing the structure of society. Language can be an insurmountable barrier between groups of people, but it does not require an entirely different tongue (or lack thereof) to separate people into groups. To be able to communicate means to be able to share and receive information, news, and, most importantly, ideas. To deprive a human of that ability, or even to slightly curtail it, is a fundamental form of oppression, without ideas and relationships humans are no more than any other animal. Thus, limiting the communication of others results is a way of expressing power over them. That which can not be communicated with fundamentally falls into the unknown, which is an element that any adventure requires. An adventure inherently relies upon incompleteness of information, for otherwise the task is trivialized.
The phrase “to silence” implies that the subject of silencing must have, at some point, been able to speak. However, the concept of silence is not dependent upon the act of silencing. An example of a subject that was never able to speak would be the moths in Zagajewski’s poem of the same name. The poem very clearly constructs two groups: insiders, who are referred to as “we” and “us,” and then outsiders, referred to as moths or, later in the poem, a more sinister “you.” The very first thing that the poem accomplishes is a separation between the narrator and the moths by having one group act on the other: the moths “watched us” (Zagajewski, 1) which immediately tells the reader that the moths are a separate group. Similar language is used throughout the poem, every verb defines the groups. For example, the narrator says that his group was “skewered,” a word with harmful connotation, “by their lambent gazes” (Zagajewski, 3). All descriptions come from a single party, the fact that the moths never get to express anything viewpoints for themselves makes it very easy to construct an us-versus-them dichotomy. The silent nature of the moths allows the narrator to shape them into whatever form he pleases. While there is nothing inherently harmful about the moths’ desire to come inside, the one-sided report turns them into monsters.

Conversely, Ellison’s The Invisible Man shows the other side of a similar dichotomy. His invisibility, and by extension, his silence, comes not from a physical property but from the perception of others around him. He notes that onlookers, instead of perceiving him, see “everything and anything except me” (Ellison, 1). This state of disconnection makes him question if he’s just a “a figure in a nightmare which the sleeper tries with all his strength to destroy” (Ellison, 1) which in turn creates a sense of resentment. With involuntary invisibility there comes an inherent desire to be seen, but Ellison thinks that to be seen he must fill the role that those who consider him invisible have created for him as part of their nightmares: “you begin to bump people back” (Ellison, 1). He recounts nearly murdering a man out of desire to be seen, suggesting that the reader would see him as “a horrible, irresponsible bastard” (Ellison, 2) and entirely agreeing, which suggests that he accepts the ethical framework given to him. However, the same silence that subjugates him absolves him (at least in his own mind), he asks “to whom can I be responsible, and why should I be, when you refuse to see me?” (Ellison, 2). Justifying his actions in this manner, focusing on responsibility rather than the act itself, provides additional evidence that he believes he must play a character. The narrator frames recognition as a prerequisite to responsibility, specifically that “Responsibility rests upon recognition” and prior to that he refers to “agreement” which is likely social contract (Ellison, 2). The narrator has quite a conundrum, he can be recognized only if he meets the abhorrent expectations but otherwise he loses all prospects of being seen. That results in a separation from self, he has a concept of what he should be and then there is the (still invisible) monster, who “should have used [his] knife to protect the higher interests of society,” (Ellison 3) doing even more to meet the image made for him. He concludes that “the invisible victim is responsible for the fate of all,” (Ellison, 3) drawing attention to both his victimhood and his purpose within the system that attempts to reject him.

Colson’s Underground Railroad demonstrates othering on a far greater scale. To enslave any group requires believing that they are inferior, but no one wants to rely on the suffering of others.
